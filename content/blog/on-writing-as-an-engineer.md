I've been meaning to write publicly for years. The excuses were always the same: not enough time, nothing original to say, imposter syndrome dressed up as humility.

Then I realized something. The engineers I admire most — the ones who shaped how I think about security, about systems, about craft — almost all of them write. Not because they have all the answers, but because writing *is* how they find the answers.

## Writing Is Thinking

There's a particular kind of clarity that only comes from trying to explain something to someone who isn't in your head. You think you understand a topic until you sit down to write about it, and suddenly all the gaps reveal themselves.

I've lost count of the number of times I started drafting a blog post about a technique, only to realize halfway through that my understanding was incomplete. The post didn't just communicate knowledge — it *created* it.

## The Bar Is Lower Than You Think

You don't need to write the definitive guide to anything. A well-written account of how you debugged a weird issue is genuinely useful. A short reflection on a tool you tried is valuable. Even a half-formed thought, clearly articulated, can spark a conversation that leads somewhere interesting.

The internet has plenty of polished, SEO-optimized content. What it lacks is honest, specific writing from people doing real work.

## So Here We Are

This blog is my commitment to thinking in public. Some posts will be technical deep-dives. Some will be half-baked opinions. Some will have nothing to do with tech at all.

If even one post helps someone solve a problem, or makes them feel less alone in the weird experience of building software, that's more than enough.
